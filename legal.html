Republic of Madagascar
Land Law in Madagascar
Malagasy land tenure is among the most complex in Sub-Saharan Africa, historically shaped by French colonial law.

All land in Madagascar that is not registered or privately titled is presumed to belong to the State. Foreign nationals cannot own land directly but may access it through long-term leases of up to 99 years.

Constitutional Framework

The 2010 Constitution of Madagascar recognizes the right to property for Malagasy citizens. Foreign ownership of land is expressly prohibited by law. The state retains sovereign ownership over all unregistered land under the principle of domanialité.

Since the 2005–2008 land reforms, Madagascar has modernized its system by introducing Certificats Fonciers (land certificates) as an alternative to the formal title deed, issued at the municipal level.

Republic of Kenya
Land Law in Kenya
Kenya’s 2010 Constitution transformed land law with a three-tier classification system and strong citizenship protections.

Kenya classifies land as public, community, or private. Foreigners may only hold land under leasehold (maximum 99 years). The Constitution explicitly prohibits freehold land ownership by non-citizens.

Constitutional Framework

Article 65 of the 2010 Constitution restricts ownership of freehold land in Kenya to citizens only. Any land held by a non-citizen is automatically converted to leasehold on a term not exceeding 99 years. The National Land Commission, established under Article 67, manages public land and oversees historical land injustices.

Kenya’s land classification system distinguishes between public land (owned by the government), community land (owned by communities under customary law), and private land (individual freehold or leasehold).

Citizen Rights

  • Full freehold ownership available
  • Leasehold (government land: 99 years max)
  • Community land rights under customary law
  • Right to mortgage, sell, or bequeath

Foreign National Rights

  • Freehold automatically converted to leasehold
  • Cannot hold community land
  • Foreign companies may lease, not own
  • Spouse of citizen may not inherit freehold

Land Dispute Resolution

Kenya’s Environment and Land Court (ELC), established under Article 162(2)(b) of the Constitution, has exclusive jurisdiction over land and environment disputes. The court operates across all counties with specialized magistrates.

Historical Land Injustices The National Land Commission is mandated under Section 15 of the NLC Act to investigate historical land injustices, including colonial-era forced displacements. Claims may be submitted to the Commission for review and restitution.

United Republic of Tanzania
Land Law in Tanzania
Tanzania operates one of the most restrictive foreign land ownership regimes in Africa — all land belongs to the state.

In Tanzania, all land is public property vested in the President as trustee for the citizens. No individual — citizen or foreigner — can own land in fee simple. Rights of occupancy are granted by the government.

Constitutional Framework

Tanzania’s land ownership model is rooted in the Arusha Declaration of 1967, which nationalized all land. The 1995 Land Policy reaffirmed that all land is public land held in trust by the President. Citizens hold land through a right of occupancy — a leasehold interest granted for 33, 66, or 99 years, renewable upon expiry.

Foreigners can only access land for investment purposes under a “derivative right” sub-leased from a citizen or the Tanzania Investment Centre (TIC).

Zanzibar Special Regime

Zanzibar (comprising Unguja and Pemba islands) has its own land laws under the Zanzibar Land Tenure Act No. 12 of 1992. Foreigners may acquire a Certificate of Granted Right of Occupancy for up to 33 years for approved residential or tourism projects via ZIPA.

Village Land & Investment Conflicts Large-scale agricultural investments in Tanzania have historically led to conflicts with village communities regarding the conversion of village land for foreign investor use. The Land Acquisition Act (Cap. 118) governs compulsory acquisition and requires fair and prompt compensation.

Republic of Mauritius
Land Law in Mauritius
Mauritius offers the most open foreign land ownership framework in the region, especially through structured investment schemes.

Mauritius uniquely allows foreigners to purchase property and acquire permanent residency through approved investment schemes. However, general freehold land purchase by non-citizens remains restricted outside designated programmes.

Constitutional Framework

The Constitution of Mauritius (1968) protects the right to property under Section 8. Mauritius follows a hybrid civil law and common law system inherited from French and British colonial influences. The Civil Code of Mauritius (based on the Napoleonic Code) governs most property transactions.

Mauritius has progressively liberalized its property regime through the Integrated Resort Scheme (IRS), Real Estate Scheme (RES), and Property Development Scheme (PDS) as the main gateways for foreign property ownership.

Foreign Investment Schemes

The Property Development Scheme (PDS), which replaced IRS and RES in 2015, allows foreigners to purchase residential units of at least USD 375,000, qualifying them for Mauritian Permanent Residency. Developers must allocate 25% of land to social housing or contribute to a social amenities fund.

The Smart City Scheme allows foreigners to purchase residential and commercial units in approved smart city developments, with residency benefits linked to investment thresholds above USD 500,000. The G+2 Scheme allows non-citizens to purchase apartments in buildings of at least three stories.

Citizen Rights

  • Full freehold ownership rights
  • Purchase any land or property freely
  • Entitled to agricultural land acquisition
  • No approval required for local purchases
  • Subject to standard registration duties

Foreign National Rights

  • PDS units ≥ USD 375,000 (+ residency)
  • Smart City commercial/residential units
  • G+2 apartments in approved buildings
  • Leasehold for industrial use via EDB
  • Requires Economic Development Board approval
Residency Benefit A foreigner purchasing property through PDS, Smart City, or similar schemes at the minimum threshold automatically qualifies for a Permanent Residence Permit in Mauritius, valid for 20 years and renewable.

Republic of South Africa
Land Law in South Africa
South Africa has a dual challenge: an open property market for foreigners alongside an ongoing land reform debate driven by historical dispossession.

South Africa currently allows foreigners to own land on the same basis as citizens, in freehold. However, land reform legislation — including potential expropriation without compensation — remains under active political and legal debate.

Constitutional Framework

Section 25 of the Constitution of South Africa, 1996 (the “Property Clause”) protects individuals against arbitrary deprivation of property but also empowers the state to expropriate for public purposes, subject to just and equitable compensation. It further mandates land reform to redress historical inequalities caused by apartheid-era dispossession.

The Expropriation Act 13 of 2024 allows in limited circumstances for expropriation at nil compensation for unused or abandoned land held for speculative purposes. The Act remains contested and is subject to constitutional challenges.

Citizen Rights

  • Full freehold ownership rights
  • Eligible for land restitution claims
  • Access to state land redistribution
  • Right to mortgage and bequeath freely
  • Protected against arbitrary dispossession

Foreign National Rights

  • Same freehold rights as citizens (currently)
  • No restrictions on residential purchases
  • Agricultural land purchase without restriction
  • Subject to same transfer duty as residents
  • Watch: proposed foreign ownership caps

Land Reform Programme

South Africa’s land reform programme has three pillars: restitution (returning land lost after 1913 to dispossessed communities), redistribution (transferring farmland through willing-seller/willing-buyer and state grants), and tenure reform (securing rights of farm workers and labour tenants). Since 1994, over 8 million hectares have been redistributed or restored.

Transfer Duties & Costs

Transfer duty is payable on property transactions above R1,100,000 (2024 threshold), on a progressive scale from 3% to 13%. Properties below this threshold are exempt. VAT at 15% applies where the seller is a VAT vendor, in which case transfer duty does not also apply.

Expropriation Act 2024 — Key Context The Expropriation Act 13 of 2024 signed by President Ramaphosa in January 2025 does not amount to blanket land grabs. Nil compensation is only envisaged in specific, narrowly defined circumstances — such as abandoned land held purely speculatively. The Act still requires due process and is subject to constitutional court scrutiny.
